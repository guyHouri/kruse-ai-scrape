# Golden Deconstruction Examples

Use these examples to learn the editorial boundary. Do not copy wording unless
the source item actually supports it. The goal is taste: novelty, mechanism,
evidence, and usable implications.

## Good Card Pattern: Cancer Fat-Burning Rebuttal

Why it worked:

- It was not "Kruse says keto is bad." It identified a specific debate:
  Seyfried / Warburg glucose-fermentation framing versus tumors that actively
  use fatty-acid oxidation.
- It named the mechanism: CPT1A, beta-oxidation, OXPHOS, lipid-rich tumor
  microenvironments.
- It cited concrete studies and explained what each study changes.
- It gave the reader a useful implication: "starve cancer with keto" can be
  dangerously overbroad because some cancers weaponize fat metabolism.

Bad version to reject:

> Cancer is mitochondrial and keto misses the picture. Kruse connects tumor
> metabolism to decentralized medicine.

Why bad: vague, no mechanism, no papers, no protocol risk.

## Good Card Pattern: Blood NAD Test Critique

Why it worked:

- It found the new angle: blood NAD measurements may miss the tissue
  compartment where NR/NMN pathways act.
- It explained why: red blood cells and white blood cells lack the relevant
  uptake machinery; damaged tissues express the repair genes.
- It translated the mechanism into a practical diagnostic warning: blood tests
  can look impressive while missing the tissue biology.

Bad version to reject:

> NAD is important for mitochondria and blood tests are not everything.

Why bad: the user already knows NAD and mitochondria matter. The value is in
the compartment mismatch and the mechanism.

## Good Card Pattern: Glycolysis As Isotope Filter

Why it worked:

- It did not just repeat "deuterium matters." It reframed glycolysis as a
  hydrogen-isotope sorting step.
- It connected biochemistry to the Kruse thesis through a specific mechanism:
  deuterium handling, mitochondrial nanomotors, and water chemistry.
- It gave the reader a new mental model rather than a slogan.

Bad version to reject:

> Deuterium harms mitochondria, so people should eat seafood and get sun.

Why bad: evergreen Kruse basics, no new source-specific detail.

## Bad Card Pattern: Name-Drop Without Payoff

Reject cards like:

> Claude Shannon's work underpins decentralized medicine because information
> theory describes communication and entropy.

Why bad:

- It only connects a famous person or field to Kruse.
- It gives no new protocol, no new case, no new research paper, no measurable
  effect, and no practical implication.
- "X supports Kruse" is not enough. Explain what X shows and how it changes
  interpretation or action.

## Bad Card Pattern: Known Basics

Reject cards like:

> Eating within 30 minutes of sunrise anchors leptin signaling.

Why bad:

- Sunrise, blue blockers, cold, DHA, seafood, grounding, nnEMF avoidance,
  Leptin Rx basics, and "get outside" are baseline knowledge for this reader.
- Keep them only when the source adds a new parameter, mechanism, evidence,
  case result, risk, or geopolitical/environmental implication.

## Bad Card Pattern: Jargon Lead

Reject or rewrite leads like:

> Outer Rotating Body Inertia Tensor Predicts Post-Transition Pole Geometry.

Why bad:

- A lay reader cannot tell what changed or why it matters.
- If the idea is worth keeping, translate first: what physical system, what
  mechanism, what evidence, and what practical implication?

## Forum Standard

Good forum updates are allowed when they have one of:

- Jack Kruse posting a meaningful new mechanism, case interpretation, protocol
  suggestion, or environmental/geographic claim.
- A member case with enough concrete detail to teach a pattern.
- A forum discussion that clarifies a current tweet/podcast/update.

Drop forum chatter, thanks, setup issues, one-line affirmations, and member
posts without a usable case or mechanism.
